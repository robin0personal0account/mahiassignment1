Microeconomics Overview

1. Definition of Microeconomics

Microeconomics is a field of economics that focuses on the behavior of individuals, households, and businesses as they make decisions within the economy.

It contrasts with macroeconomics by zeroing in on smaller individual components.

It examines how choices and incentives influence the allocation and use of resources at a personal or business level.

Microeconomics explains why different goods and services hold varying values and how businesses and individuals engage in efficient production and trade, collaborating for mutual benefit.

2. Scope of Microeconomics

  • Factor Markets: Studies how labor, capital, and other inputs are allocated and priced, including wage determination and capital markets.
  • Welfare Economics: Evaluates how economic policies and market outcomes affect social welfare, focusing on resource and income distribution.
  • Market Failures and Government Intervention: Examines cases where markets fail, such as externalities and public goods, and the role of government intervention.
  • Behavioral Economics: Analyzes how psychological factors influence economic decision-making, deviating from traditional rational models.

3. Limitations of Microeconomics

  • Unrealistic Assumptions: Assumes "ceteris paribus," meaning all other factors remain constant, which rarely happens in practice.
  • Static Analysis: Uses static analysis that may not apply to real-world dynamic scenarios.
  • Full Employment Assumption: Often assumes full employment, which is not realistic in all contexts.
  • Laissez-Faire Policy Assumption: Assumes minimal government intervention, but real-world governments are usually involved in economic regulation.
  • Neglect of Macroeconomic Factors: Focuses narrowly on specific segments of the economy and overlooks the larger macroeconomic picture.

4. Economic Goods vs Free Goods

Economic goods are limited in supply and require human effort, while free goods, like air, are abundant and available without effort.

  • Economic Goods: Limited, involve opportunity costs, and are exchanged in markets.
  • Free Goods: Abundant, do not involve opportunity costs, and are naturally available without the need for human effort.

5. Production Possibility Curve (PPC)

The Production Possibility Curve (PPC) illustrates the trade-offs between producing two goods using limited resources. It shows the maximum potential output for each good.

The curve demonstrates opportunity costs, as increasing the production of one good requires sacrificing the production of another.

Example:

  • At point B: 20,000 oranges and 120,000 apples.
  • At point C: 45,000 oranges and 85,000 apples.

The curve is outward-bending, reflecting the trade-offs involved. The highest point on the curve represents production of one good and none of the other.
